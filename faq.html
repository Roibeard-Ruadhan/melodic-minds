Frequently Asked Questions

What is music therapy?

Music therapy is the use of music to improve physical, emotional, cognitive, and social functioning. It is a therapeutic modality that can be used with individuals of all ages and abilities.

What happens during a music therapy session?

During a music therapy session, a trained music therapist will use music to address specific goals or needs of the individual or group. This may include singing, playing instruments, movement to music, or listening to music. The therapist may also use discussion and reflection to help process the experience.

Who can benefit from music therapy?

Music therapy can benefit individuals of all ages and abilities, including those with physical, emotional, cognitive, or social challenges. It has been used successfully in a variety of settings, including hospitals, schools, nursing homes, and community programs.

Do I need to have musical experience to participate in music therapy?

No musical experience is necessary to participate in music therapy. The music therapist will tailor the session to the needs and abilities of the individual or group.

How long is a typical music therapy session?

The length of a music therapy session can vary depending on the individual and their specific goals. Sessions may range from 30 minutes to an hour or more, and may be held weekly, bi-weekly, or on a more frequent basis. The therapist will work with the client to determine the most appropriate length and frequency of sessions.

What qualifications do music therapists have?

Music therapists are required to have a bachelor's or master's degree in music therapy from an accredited program, as well as complete a supervised clinical internship and pass a board certification exam. They must also participate in ongoing professional development and adhere to a code of ethics. It is important to work with a credentialed music therapist to ensure quality and effective treatment.
